# Netflix Walked Away from Warner Bros. That Might Be the Best Product Decision of the Year.

The entertainment industry has been buzzing about Netflix's decision to back out of a potential merger with Warner Bros. Shareholders cheered. Analysts weighed in. And somewhere in a group chat, two people who clearly pay attention had a conversation that stuck with me.

Here's what I think this move tells us: not just about Netflix, but about product strategy, storytelling, and what it really means to build something that lasts.

## Netflix Doesn't Need Warner Bros. to Win

Let's be clear. Netflix is already profitable. It doesn't need an acquisition to survive. Walking away from Warner Bros. means walking away from its debt, its legacy studio politics, and the expectations that come bundled with decades-old IP.

What Netflix keeps in return is something far more valuable: **creative freedom**. The ability to greenlight *Bridgerton*, *Stranger Things*, *Love Is Blind*, and yes, even *K-Pop Demon Hunters* without a boardroom of traditionalists second-guessing every decision. That flexibility is a product moat that's genuinely hard to replicate.

For product managers, this is a classic **build vs. buy** decision. And Netflix chose to keep building.

## The "Short-Lived" Problem Is Real, and It's a Product Problem

Here's where it gets interesting. Even with creative freedom, Netflix has a reputation problem: **its hits feel short-lived**. Shows blow up, dominate the cultural conversation for two weeks, then vanish.

Is that a content problem? Partly. But I think it's also a **product and incentive problem**.

Writers and producers are usually laser-focused on selling a strong first season, because getting *anything* made is so difficult. The long-term arc comes second, if at all. Unless you're someone like Michael Schur, who had a clear end-to-end vision for *The Good Place* and the leverage to protect it, you're mostly at the mercy of the studio. Multi-season plans get sidelined. Long arcs get forgotten.

Netflix's algorithm rewards engagement spikes. That creates a system optimized for *hooks*, not *depth*.

## The Warner Bros. IP Gap and What Netflix Should Do About It

This is the real lesson from the failed merger. Warner Bros. has *classics*: properties that people return to, reference, and pass down. Netflix has seasonal hits.

There's a difference between a show that trends and a show that *endures*.

If Netflix wants to close that gap, the answer isn't acquisition. It's **intentional investment in multi-season storytelling**. **Committing to one or two flagship projects built with a full narrative arc from day one.** Giving creators the runway and the protection to actually execute a vision across multiple seasons.

That's how you stop chasing trends and start building a canon.

## What This Means If You're Thinking About Product

Netflix's move is a reminder that **strategic restraint is a product decision**. Saying no to a merger, even a big splashy one, can be the right call when it protects what makes your product work.

The questions worth asking:

- Are we optimizing for short-term engagement or long-term loyalty?
- Do our incentive structures reward creators for depth, or just for launch performance?
- What's the difference between what our users *click on* and what they actually *remember*?

Netflix has the creative pipeline. Now it needs the product strategy to make its work stick.

The classics aren't built in a season. They're built with intention.
